Grace Brewster Murray Hopper
(1906 – 1992)

Rear Admiral Grace M. Hopper is one of the first programmers of the Harvard Mark 1 computer, invented the first compiler for a computer programming language, and helped develop the idea of programming languages independent of particular machines – which led to the development of COBOL. Which in turn led to another of her nicknames: Grand Old Lady of Software, Grandma COBOL, an early High Level Programming Language still in use today.

Hopper is even credited with popularizing the word “debugging” for fixing computer programming glitches, the idea of machine-independent programming languages.

Here's a time line of Grace Murray Hopper's life:

She was called by some “Amazing Grace.”

And that's because she was so gosh-darned accomplished!

  • December 9, 1906 - Born in New York. Her mother enjoyed mathematics. Her father worked as an insurance broker and had a large book collection. As a child, she built odd things with her Struktiron kit of beams, nuts, and bolts, what today we call Erector sets or Legos. She also followed her grandfather, a surveyor for New York City, as he laid out new streets, often holding the pole needed to measure distances.
  • 1913 - She was curious about how her alarm clocked worked. But when she took it apart, she was unable to put it back together. She continued taking apart seven alarm clocks, to the displeasure of her mother, who limited her to taking apart just one.
  • 1923-1928 - She graduated Vassar College, Hopper indulged her mathematical interests, and also took courses in physics and engineering. Then she attended Yale.
  • 1930 - She received a master's degree in Yale and a doctorate in 1934. These were rare achievements, especially for a woman.
  • 1931 - Hopper began teaching mathematics at Vassar College. Vassar College hired her first as an instructor, then as a professor of mathematics in 1941.
  • 1930-1945 - She lived with her husband, Vincent Foster Hopper, whom she had married in 1930. They were divorced in 1945 and had no children. She did not marry again, but chose to retain his surname.
  • 1943 - Hopper obtained a leave of absence from Vassar and was sworn into the United States Navy Reserve.
  • 1944 - 1944, and was assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard University as a lieutenant, junior grade. She started working with Howard Aiken on the Harvard Mark I computer.
  • 1945 - Grace Hopper was at Harvard University, while working on the Mark-II, she discovered a hardware failure caused by a trapped moth. She coined the now common term "bug" to refer to unexpected computer failures.
  • 1949 - Hopper became an employee of the Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation as a senior mathematician and joined the team developing the UNIVAC I. She designed an improved compiler and was part of the team which developed Flow-Matic, the first English-language data processing compiler. She invented the language APT and verified the language COBOL. The UNIVAC was the first known large-scale electronic computer to be on the market in 1950, and was more competitive at processing information than the Mark I.
  • 1952 - When Hopper recommended the development of a new programming language that would use entirely English words, she "was told very quickly that [she] couldn't do this because computers didn't understand English." Her idea was not accepted for 3 years, and she published her first paper on the subject, compilers, in 1952. Her compiler "translated mathematical notation into machine code. That was the beginning of COBOL, a computer language for data processors. She could say "Subtract income tax from pay" instead of trying to write that in octal code or using all kinds of symbols. COBOL is the major language used today in data processing."
  • 1954 - Hopper was named the company's first director of automatic programming, and her department released some of the first compiler-based programming languages, including MATH-MATIC and FLOW-MATIC.
  • 1957 - Hopper developed FLOW-MATIC, the first commercial, data-processing compiler that allowed computers to be used for automated billing and payroll calculation. FLOW-MATIC became the foundation for Hopper's next development the computer language COmmon Business Oriented Language, or COBOL.
  • 1959 - Hopper developed COBOL. Whereas IBM's FORTRAN programming language used a highly condensed, mathematical code, COBOL used common English language words. COBOL was written for use on different computers and was intended to be independent of any one computer company. For her wide-reaching influence on COBOL's development, Hopper was deemed the "grandmother of COBOL."
  • 1967-1977 - Hopper served as the director of the Navy Programming Languages Group in the Navy's Office of Information Systems Planning and was promoted to the rank of captain in 1973. She developed validation software for COBOL and its compiler as part of a COBOL standardization program for the entire Navy.
  • 1969 - Hopper was the first computer science "Man of the Year".
  • 1991 - President George H. Bush awarded her the National Medal of Technology.
  • January 1 1992 - She died a year later, in Arlington, Virginia. and was buried with military honors in Arlington National Cemetery in Virginia.

Grace's spirit is embodied by her famous quote:

"It's easier to ask forgiveness, than it is to get permission"
